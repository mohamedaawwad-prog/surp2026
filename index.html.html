Research Abstract

Project Overview

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly proposed as decision agents in supply chain planning, procurement, and logistics systems. However, existing research primarily demonstrates single-model implementations without systematic cross-model benchmarking, standardized evaluation protocols, or quantitative validation across operational scenarios.


This project develops a structured and reproducible benchmarking framework to evaluate multiple LLMs on defined supply chain decision tasks including inventory control, disruption response, and supplier selection (to be confirmed following the initial literature review). A key component is the creation of supply chain LLM agents inspired by the multi-agent consensus-seeking approach of Jannelli et al. (2025), implemented through no-code/low-code platforms such as Make.com or Python/LangChain.

The Gap This Project Addresses

Rooted in the Literature

What Jannelli et al. (2025) call for

The Identified Gap

Jannelli et al. (2025) identify in Section 2.4 the absence of supply chain-specific LLM benchmarking environments as a critical gap. Their work uses multiple foundation models but lacks a structured, reproducible cross-model evaluation protocol with standardized prompts and baseline comparisons.

This project contributes

What We Build and Add

Building on Jannelli et al. (2025): (1) design and test SC LLM agent frameworks; (2) compare multiple LLMs with standardized prompts and metrics; (3) evaluate consistency, cost impact, and interpretability across models; (4) as a secondary objective, extend the iSCDT framework (Kramer and Awwad, 2025) with empirical LLM performance evidence.

Preliminary Research Questions

Guiding Questions for the Study

Research Question 1 — Primary

How do different large language models compare in performance when used as decision agents for operational supply chain tasks — in terms of decision quality, output consistency, reasoning transparency, and cost implications relative to established baseline heuristics?

Research Question 2 — Methodological

What constitutes a standardized, reproducible benchmarking protocol for evaluating LLM performance on structured supply chain decision tasks, and how should prompt design, scenario variation, and evaluation rubrics be structured to enable fair cross-model comparison?

Research Question 3 — Agent Design

To what extent can supply chain LLM agents — built using no-code platforms or Python/LangChain and inspired by the consensus-seeking architecture of Jannelli et al. (2025) — outperform standalone LLM chat responses on the same supply chain decision tasks?

Research Question 4 — Integration (Secondary)

How do the empirical LLM benchmarking findings of this project inform the feasibility of integrating LLM agents as decision components within the iSCDT framework introduced by Kramer and Awwad (2025)?

The Bullwhip Effect

A Key Phenomenon — Already Studied, Still Relevant

The bullwhip effect — amplification of demand variability as orders move upstream — has been studied in the LLM context by Jannelli et al. (2025). For this project it is an outcome variable, not a decision task. LLM inventory decisions cause or mitigate the bullwhip effect, and it will be used as an outcome measure when evaluating inventory management decisions.

Student Research Guide

Literature Review, Zotero, VOSviewer and Conference Abstract Writing

A step-by-step guide: conducting a literature review, using AI tools for synthesis, bibliometric analysis with VOSviewer, and writing a conference abstract for IISE 2027.

What is a Literature Review?

A literature review is the intellectual foundation of your research — a synthesis that tells a coherent story about what is known, what is contested, and most importantly, what is missing. It is not a one-paper-at-a-time summary.

It drives your experimental design, metric selection, LLM choices, and interpretation of results. Every non-obvious claim in your paper needs a citation — and citations come from reading.

1

Define your bodies of knowledge

Extract keyword groups from the research questions:

  • LLM decision agents: "large language models supply chain," "LLM inventory control," "agentic AI operations"
  • Supply chain tasks: "inventory control benchmarking," "disruption response AI," "supplier selection," "bullwhip effect," "supply chain risk"
  • Benchmarking: "AI benchmarking framework," "LLM evaluation metrics," "foundation model comparison"
  • Agent frameworks: "multi-agent supply chain," "LangGraph inventory," "Make.com LLM automation"
Use these in Google Scholar, Scopus, Web of Science, and arXiv.org. Cal Poly Library OneSearch gives full-text access to most journals.
2

Collect reliable sources

  • Peer-reviewed journal and conference articles (primary)
  • arXiv preprints from established research groups
  • Textbooks — especially Ravindran and Warsing for supply chain fundamentals
  • Industry white papers (IISE, McKinsey, Gartner on AI in supply chains)
  • Platform documentation: Make.com, LangGraph, n8n
If a paper is paywalled, check arXiv first. For others, use Cal Poly Interlibrary Loan (ILL).
3

Set up Zotero — your reference manager

Zotero is free and integrates with Microsoft Word. Use it from Day 1.

Installation

  1. Go to zotero.org and download the desktop app.
  2. Create a free account and sign in — library syncs automatically.
  3. Install the Zotero Connector browser extension for Chrome or Edge. One click imports any reference from any webpage.
  4. Create collection: "SURP 2026 — LLMs Supply Chain" with sub-collections: Core Papers, LLM Evaluation, Supply Chain Tasks, Agent Frameworks, Textbooks.
  5. Set citation style to APA 7th Edition in Zotero preferences.

4

Leverage AI tools for synthesis — critically

NotebookLM (Google) — Recommended for synthesis

  1. Go to notebooklm.google.com and create a notebook for this project.
  2. Upload PDFs of your key papers.
  3. Ask: "What methods do these papers use to evaluate LLM performance?" or "What gaps do they identify?"
  4. Use summaries as a starting point — verify everything against the original paper before citing.

Other useful tools

  • ResearchRabbit: Visualize citation networks — discover related papers.
  • SciSpace: Interactive AI explanations of dense technical papers.
  • Connected Papers / Litmaps: Citation neighborhood mapping.
  • Claude (claude.ai): Explain technical concepts, help understand methodology sections, assist with writing drafts.
AI tools can hallucinate — inventing citations or misrepresenting findings. Always verify against the original document. Never cite a paper based solely on an AI summary. Dr. Awwad will check your citations.
5

Synthesize — connect, do not just summarize

Ask these questions across all sources:

  • Patterns: What findings appear consistently across 3 or more sources?
  • Gaps: Where does the literature show missing data or unexplored combinations? Section 2.4 of Jannelli et al. (2025) is a model example.
  • Translation: What proven methods could be adapted to this project?
  • Connection: How do findings support or challenge your experimental design?
Use NotebookLM to draft initial synthesis, then rewrite entirely in your own words. The literature review must read as your intellectual contribution.

Bibliometric visualization with VOSviewer

VOSviewer creates visual maps of keyword co-occurrence. This figure will appear in the conference paper and SURP poster.

Download and install

  1. Go to vosviewer.com and download for your OS. Java required — download from java.com if prompted.

Workflow: Zotero to VOSviewer

  1. In Zotero: File, Export Library, RIS format, save to OneDrive.
  2. Open VOSviewer, Create map based on bibliographic data, Read from RIS file.
  3. Choose Co-occurrence, Keywords (author keywords). Set minimum occurrence to 2.
  4. Uncheck generic terms like "study," "review," "analysis."
  5. Click Finish. Color clusters are topic groups. Node size is keyword frequency.
  6. Export as PNG and save to OneDrive Figures folder. Save .json map file for later.

Conference Writing

How to Write a Conference Abstract

The IISE 2027 abstract will be your first public academic submission. It must be precise, complete, and compelling in a small space — typically 250 to 500 words.

A

What is a conference abstract?

A conference abstract summarizes your research and is submitted for review by a program committee. If accepted, you present at the conference. The abstract must stand alone — a reader who sees only the abstract must understand what you did, why it matters, and what you found. For IISE, it is the primary artifact reviewed before your presentation.

B

The five-part structure

  1. Motivation and Context (1-2 sentences): Why does this problem matter? Example: "Supply chain management increasingly relies on AI-driven decision support, yet no standardized framework exists for comparing large language model performance across operational tasks."
  2. Problem Statement and Gap (1-2 sentences): What specific gap does your work address? Ground this in Jannelli et al. (2025). Example: "Existing research demonstrates single-model LLM implementations but lacks systematic cross-model benchmarking with standardized evaluation protocols."
  3. Approach and Methods (3-5 sentences): What did you do? Describe the benchmarking framework, decision tasks, LLMs evaluated, baselines, and agent-building component. Name the specific models and tasks.
  4. Results and Findings (2-4 sentences): What did you find? Include specific results — which model performed best, how consistent were outputs, how did agents compare to standalone responses.
  5. Conclusions and Implications (1-2 sentences): What does it mean for practitioners and researchers? How does it connect to iSCDT or broader AI-in-operations work?
Draft the abstract from your Week 7 paper. The abstract is the last thing you write — after the paper is complete. Every sentence must reflect content that exists in the paper.
C

Abstract quality checklist

  • Does it clearly state the research gap?
  • Are the specific LLMs and decision tasks named?
  • Are evaluation metrics described, even briefly?
  • Are key findings stated with at least one specific result?
  • Is the word count within the IISE limit? (Check iise.org for 2027 guidelines.)
  • Is writing in active voice, past tense for completed work?
  • Has Dr. Awwad reviewed and approved it before submission?
D

Example abstract skeleton

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly proposed as autonomous decision agents in supply chain management, yet no systematic benchmarking framework exists for comparing their performance across standardized operational tasks. This gap — explicitly identified by Jannelli et al. (2025) — limits evidence-based guidance for practitioners. This study develops and applies a reproducible benchmarking framework to evaluate [X] large language models — including [list models] — on [2-3] supply chain decision tasks: [inventory replenishment], [disruption response], and [supplier selection]. Using structured prompts through both chat interfaces and automated agent workflows built in [platform], each model was evaluated against established baselines using metrics including decision quality, output consistency, reasoning transparency, and cost impact. Results indicate that [Model A] demonstrated [finding] on [Task 1], while [Model B] outperformed others in [Task 2]. Output consistency varied significantly — consistent with limitations noted by Jannelli et al. (2025). LLM agents produced [more/less] consistent decisions than standalone chat responses. These findings provide the first systematic cross-model comparison on structured supply chain tasks and offer guidance for embedding LLM agents within intelligent supply chain digital twin architectures (Kramer and Awwad, 2025).
This is a skeleton, not a template to copy. Replace every bracketed element with your actual findings. Dr. Awwad will review it before submission.
