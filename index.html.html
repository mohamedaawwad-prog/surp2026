Research Abstract

Project Overview

Adapted from the approved SURP 2026 research proposal (Awwad, 2026)

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly proposed as decision agents in supply chain planning, procurement, and logistics systems. However, existing research primarily demonstrates single-model implementations without systematic cross-model benchmarking, standardized evaluation protocols, or quantitative validation across operational scenarios.


This project develops a structured and reproducible benchmarking framework to evaluate multiple large language models on defined supply chain decision tasks — specifically inventory control, disruption response, and supplier selection (to be confirmed following an initial literature review). Using consistent input conditions and scenario variations, models will be assessed against operational baselines using measurable performance metrics including cost impact, service level effects, and output consistency.


A key component of the project is the creation of supply chain LLM agents — structured, prompt-driven decision frameworks inspired by the multi-agent consensus-seeking approach of Jannelli et al. (2025) — implemented through no-code/low-code platforms such as Make.com, Python/LangChain, or equivalent tools. By providing empirical comparison across models, the project advances methodological rigor in evaluating generative AI for industrial engineering and directly extends the Intelligent Supply Chain Digital Twin (iSCDT) framework (Kramer & Awwad, 2025).

The Gap This Project Addresses

Rooted in the Literature

What Jannelli et al. (2025) explicitly call for

The Identified Gap

Jannelli et al. (2025) demonstrate LLM-based consensus-seeking in a sequential supply chain inventory setting and explicitly identify in Section 2.4 the absence of supply chain-specific LLM benchmarking environments as a critical gap. Their work uses multiple foundation models but does not provide a structured, reproducible cross-model evaluation protocol with standardized prompts and baseline comparisons.

This project's primary contribution

What We Build and Add

Building directly on Jannelli et al. (2025), this project: (1) designs and tests supply chain LLM agent frameworks for specific decision tasks; (2) compares multiple LLMs using standardized prompts and documented metrics; (3) evaluates output consistency, cost impact, and interpretability across models; (4) as a secondary objective, extends the iSCDT framework (Kramer & Awwad, 2025) by providing empirical LLM performance evidence within that architecture.

Performance Evaluation

Key Metrics — Accessible and Actionable

Designed to be measurable by an undergraduate researcher through structured prompt logging, comparison tables, and simple cost formulas from the textbook.

📋

Decision Quality

Does the model's recommendation match what a textbook heuristic (EOQ, s,S policy, scoring rubric) would suggest? How far off is it?

🔁

Consistency

When the same prompt is submitted multiple times, does the model give the same answer? Inconsistency is a known limitation flagged by Jannelli et al. (2025).

💡

Reasoning Quality

Does the model explain its decision clearly and logically? Rate on a structured rubric. A supply chain manager must be able to understand and trust the output.

⚠️

Robustness

Does the model perform well when scenario details change — demand spikes, supplier delays, disruptions? Test with easy, medium, and hard scenario variants.

💰

Cost Impact

Using supply chain cost formulas from the textbook (inventory + backlog costs, as in Jannelli et al. 2025), estimate the financial implication of each model's recommendation vs. the baseline.

🤝

Agent Behavior

In the agent-building phase: does the LLM agent follow instructions? Does it reduce the bullwhip effect vs. a standalone response? Does it negotiate appropriately in a multi-agent setup?

Preliminary Research Questions

Guiding Questions for the Study

Research Question 1 — Primary

How do different large language models (LLMs) compare in performance when used as decision agents for operational supply chain tasks — specifically in terms of decision quality, output consistency, reasoning transparency, and cost implications relative to established baseline heuristics?

Research Question 2 — Methodological

What constitutes a standardized, reproducible benchmarking protocol for evaluating LLM performance on structured supply chain decision tasks, and how should prompt design, scenario variation, and evaluation rubrics be structured to enable fair cross-model comparison?

Research Question 3 — Agent Design

To what extent can supply chain LLM agents — built using no-code platforms (e.g., Make.com) or Python/LangChain frameworks and inspired by the consensus-seeking architecture of Jannelli et al. (2025) — outperform standalone LLM chat responses on the same supply chain decision tasks?

Research Question 4 — Integration (Secondary)

How do the empirical LLM benchmarking findings of this project inform the feasibility of integrating LLM agents as decision components within the Intelligent Supply Chain Digital Twin (iSCDT) framework introduced by Kramer and Awwad (2025)?

The Bullwhip Effect

A Key Phenomenon — Already Studied, But Still Relevant

The bullwhip effect — the amplification of demand variability as orders move upstream through a supply chain — is one of the most well-documented phenomena in supply chain management. It has already been studied in the context of LLM agents by Jannelli et al. (2025), who used it as the primary outcome variable in their inventory management case study.

For this project, the bullwhip effect should be understood as a consequence variable — not a decision task in itself. Decisions about inventory replenishment (how much to order and when) directly cause or mitigate the bullwhip effect. This project can use the bullwhip effect as an outcome measure when evaluating LLM-driven inventory decisions, while also studying novel decision contexts (disruption response, supplier selection, risk management) that have received less attention in the LLM literature.

References: Sterman (2015); Dejonckheere et al. (2004); Jannelli et al. (2025, Sections 3–6). These are core readings for understanding why good inventory decisions matter.

    Leverage AI tools to accelerate synthesis — critically

    AI tools can dramatically speed up your literature review — but they must be used carefully. They are a starting point for understanding, not a substitute for reading and thinking.

    NotebookLM (Google) — Recommended for synthesis

    NotebookLM is Dr. Awwad's recommended AI synthesis tool for this project. You upload your PDFs and other documents, and it generates cohesive summaries, answers your questions about the content, and helps you identify connections across sources.

    1. Go to notebooklm.google.com and sign in with a Google account.

    4. Ask it questions like: "What methods do these papers use to evaluate LLM performance?" or "What gaps do these papers identify for future research?"

    Other AI tools for literature discovery

    • ResearchRabbit: Visualize citation networks — see which papers cite each other and discover related work. Great for finding papers you might have missed.
    • SciSpace: Interactive AI explanations of individual papers — useful when a paper is technically dense.
    • Litmaps / Connected Papers: Citation mapping — visualize the "neighborhood" of a paper.
    • Claude (claude.ai): Good for explaining concepts, summarizing methodology sections, and helping you understand technical content. Ask it to explain the bullwhip effect, EOQ, or multi-agent consensus-seeking in plain language.
    Critical warning: AI tools can hallucinate — they may invent citations, misrepresent findings, or produce plausible-sounding but incorrect summaries. Always verify AI summaries against the original document before citing. Never cite a paper based solely on an AI summary. You must read the paper yourself.

    What is a Literature Review — and Why Does It Matter?

    A literature review is the intellectual foundation of your research. It is not a summary of individual papers — it is a synthesis that tells a coherent story about what is known, what is contested, and most importantly, what is missing (the gap your project fills).

    Primary goal: Generate insights that guide your experimental design, metric selection, LLM and task choices, and interpretation of results.

    Your literature review is the foundation for the conference paper and the SURP poster. Every non-obvious fact needs a citation — and the only way to have the right citations is to have done the reading.

    Start with the project's research questions and extract keyword groups:

    • Benchmarking methodology: "AI benchmarking framework," "LLM evaluation metrics," "prompt engineering reproducibility," "foundation model comparison"
    • Agent frameworks: "multi-agent supply chain," "LangGraph inventory," "Make.com LLM automation," "consensus-seeking agents"

    Conference Writing

    How to Write a Conference Abstract

    The IISE 2027 abstract will be your first public academic submission. It must be precise, complete, and compelling in a small space.

    A

    What is a conference abstract?

    A conference abstract is a structured summary of your research — typically 250 to 500 words — submitted for review. If accepted, you present the full work at the conference. The abstract must stand alone: a reader who sees only the abstract must understand what you did, why it matters, and what you found. For IISE, the abstract is the primary submission artifact reviewed before your presentation.

    B

    The five-part structure

    1. Motivation / Context (1–2 sentences): Why does this problem matter? Who cares, and why now? Example: "Supply chain management increasingly relies on AI-driven decision support, yet no standardized framework exists for comparing large language model performance across operational tasks."
    2. Problem Statement / Gap (1–2 sentences): What specific gap does your work address? Ground this in Jannelli et al. (2025). Example: "Existing research demonstrates single-model LLM implementations but lacks systematic cross-model benchmarking with standardized evaluation protocols."
    3. Approach / Methods (3–5 sentences): What did you do? Describe the benchmarking framework, decision tasks, LLMs evaluated, baseline comparisons, and agent-building component. Name the specific models and tasks.
    4. Results / Findings (2–4 sentences): What did you find? Include specific results — which model performed best, how consistent were outputs, how did agents compare to standalone responses.
    5. Conclusions / Implications (1–2 sentences): What does it mean for practitioners and researchers? How does it connect to iSCDT or broader AI-in-operations work?
    Draft the abstract from your Week 7 paper draft. The abstract should be the last thing you write — after the paper is complete. Every sentence must reflect content that exists in the paper.
    C

    Abstract quality checklist

    • Does the abstract clearly state the research gap being addressed?
    • Are the specific LLMs and supply chain decision tasks named?
    • Are evaluation metrics described, even briefly?
    • Are key findings stated with at least one specific result?
    • Is the word count within the IISE limit? (Check iise.org for 2027 guidelines.)
    • Is writing in active voice, past tense for completed work?
    • Has Dr. Awwad reviewed and approved it before submission?
    D

    Example abstract skeleton

    Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly proposed as autonomous decision agents in supply chain management, yet no systematic benchmarking framework exists for comparing their performance across standardized operational tasks. This gap — explicitly identified by Jannelli et al. (2025) — limits evidence-based guidance for practitioners seeking to deploy LLM agents responsibly in supply chain environments. This study develops and applies a reproducible benchmarking framework to evaluate [X] large language models — including [list LLMs] — on [2–3] canonical supply chain decision tasks: [inventory replenishment], [disruption response], and [supplier selection]. Using structured prompts submitted through both chat interfaces and automated agent workflows built in [Make.com / Python-LangChain], each model was evaluated against established operational baselines ([EOQ policy], [weighted scoring matrix]) using metrics including decision quality, output consistency, reasoning transparency, and cost impact. Results indicate that [Model A] demonstrated [finding] on [Task 1], while [Model B] outperformed others in [Task 2]. Output consistency varied significantly across models — consistent with limitations noted by Jannelli et al. (2025). LLM agents produced [more/less] consistent decisions than standalone chat responses. These findings provide the first systematic cross-model comparison of LLM performance on structured supply chain decision tasks and offer practical guidance for embedding LLM agents within intelligent supply chain digital twin architectures (Kramer & Awwad, 2025).
    This is a skeleton, not a template to copy. Replace every bracketed element with your actual findings. Dr. Awwad will review it carefully before submission.
